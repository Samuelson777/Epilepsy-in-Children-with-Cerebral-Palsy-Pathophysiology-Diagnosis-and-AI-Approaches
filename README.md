## Abstract

This repository contains the complete review paper titled **"Neural Epilepsy in Children with Cerebral Palsy: A Comprehensive Review of Pathophysiology, Diagnosis, Clinical Management, Artificial Intelligence, and Future Research Directions."**

The review synthesizes current knowledge on the epidemiology, neurobiology, genetics, molecular mechanisms, clinical manifestations, diagnostic technologies, therapeutic strategies, rehabilitation, and emerging artificial intelligence (AI) applications related to epilepsy in children with cerebral palsy (CP).

The objective is to provide clinicians, neuroscientists, biomedical engineers, rehabilitation specialists, and researchers with a comprehensive overview of the current evidence while identifying future directions for precision medicine and AI-assisted neurological care.

# Conclusion

Epilepsy remains one of the most common and clinically significant neurological comorbidities in children with cerebral palsy. The interaction between developmental brain injury, genetic susceptibility, neuroinflammation, excitotoxicity, altered neuronal connectivity, and network reorganization contributes to the development of recurrent seizures and long-term neurological impairment.

Advances in neuroimaging, electrophysiology, molecular neuroscience, and computational medicine have substantially improved our understanding of epileptogenesis. Artificial intelligence and machine learning are emerging as powerful tools for automated seizure detection, neuroimaging analysis, risk prediction, and personalized clinical decision-making. Although these technologies show considerable promise, further validation through large-scale, multicenter studies is required before widespread clinical implementation.

A multidisciplinary approach that integrates pediatric neurology, rehabilitation, genetics, biomedical engineering, and data science offers the greatest opportunity to improve diagnosis, optimize treatment, and enhance quality of life for children living with cerebral palsy and epilepsy. Continued collaboration between clinicians, researchers, and engineers will be essential to translate scientific discoveries into safe, effective, and equitable clinical practice.
